01 / What It Was

The Modular
Smartphone Dream

Project Ara was Google's audacious attempt to reinvent the personal computer cycle for smartphones. Conceived within Motorola's Advanced Technology and Projects (ATAP) group in 2013, the project envisioned a smartphone platform where every component — processor, display, camera, battery — could be swapped by the user as easily as snapping together Lego bricks.

Named after Ara Knaian, co-founder of NK Labs, one of the key engineering contractors on the project, Ara aimed to simultaneously solve three of tech's hardest problems: e-waste, exclusivity, and the relentless churn of planned obsolescence.

Google originally designed Ara to be utilized by "6 billion people" — the one billion existing smartphone users, plus the five billion still on feature phones who lacked access to flagship hardware. An entry-level skeleton "endo" frame would cost around $50, with premium modules purchased à la carte.

When Google sold Motorola Mobility to Lenovo in 2014, it retained the ATAP team and Project Ara, signalling just how seriously the company took the initiative. For three years, it consumed hundreds of engineers, millions in investment, and the imagination of the entire tech community.

02 / Engineering

How It Would've Worked

01

The Endoskeleton Frame

A structural "endo" containing core system components, power distribution rails, and a high-speed data bus. The frame was the only permanent, Google-manufactured component of the phone — the host to everything else.

02

Hot-Swappable Modules

Individual modules for camera, battery, processor, sensors, and speakers could be attached or removed without ever powering the device down — treating the phone like a desktop PC tower, but pocket-sized.

03

Greybus Protocol

Google developed a universal high-speed data protocol allowing diverse hardware modules from any manufacturer to communicate seamlessly with the main system at up to 10 Gbit/s per connection.

04

Module Marketplace

An open ecosystem where third-party developers could design, manufacture, and sell custom modules — from better cameras to medical sensors, receipt printers, laser pointers, and night-vision attachments.

Technical Innovation

Electro-Permanent Magnets

Unlike traditional magnets, these could be electrically activated to attach and release modules on demand — ensuring rock-solid connections while allowing effortless hot-swapping at any moment.

UniPro / Greybus Stack

A high-speed protocol stack allowing modules from any manufacturer to interoperate. Data transfer rates reached up to 10 Gbit/s per slot connection, with 2×2 modules achieving 20 Gbit/s.

Intelligent Power Management

A dynamic power distribution system capable of managing modules with radically different voltage and current requirements — from a low-power GPS sensor to a high-draw GPU module.

3D-Printed Modules

Google contracted 3D Systems to experiment with printing electrical components, enabling a future where consumers could design and fabricate their own specialized modules at home.

03 / Timeline

From Concept
to Cancellation

Sep 2013

Phonebloks Goes Viral

Dutch designer Dave Hakkens publishes his vision of a modular "Phonebloks" phone on YouTube. Within days, the video spreads globally, proving public appetite for the concept.

Oct 2013

Project Ara Announced

Motorola ATAP publicly announces Project Ara, inspired by Phonebloks but backed by real engineering infrastructure. Concept work had actually begun quietly in 2012.

Jan 2014

Google Takes Over

After selling Motorola to Lenovo, Google retains Project Ara and the ATAP team — a clear signal of the project's strategic importance at the highest levels of the company.

Apr 2014

First Developer Conference

Google hosts the inaugural Ara Developer Conference at the Computer History Museum, unveiling the Spiral 1 prototype and releasing the Module Developer Kit (MDK).

Jan 2015

Spiral 2 Prototype

The second-generation prototype arrives with improved design, 3G connectivity, eleven prototype modules, and the introduction of electro-permanent magnets for module attachment.

May 2016

Developer Edition Announced

Google announces plans to ship a developer edition by fall 2016, with a full consumer launch targeted for 2017. Optimism reaches its peak.

Sep 2016

Project Cancelled

Google unexpectedly announces the suspension of Project Ara, citing "business and technical challenges." No further details are offered. The modular dream is officially over.

04 / Why It Failed

The Hard
Truths

Technical Limitations

The modular design introduced significant latency, power inefficiency, and signal interference. Modern smartphones depend on tightly integrated components — something modularity inherently compromises. Multiple connection points between modules created compounding problems that were near-impossible to engineer away.

Size & Weight

The endoskeleton frame and connection mechanisms added considerable bulk. While competitors raced toward thinner, lighter designs, Ara prototypes were noticeably thicker and heavier. The prototype measured 9.7mm — already thicker than most contemporaries — and that was before power-hungry modules were attached.

Cost Overrun

Manufacturing modular components at scale proved far more expensive than anticipated. Individual modules, priced separately, would have cost the consumer more than an equivalent integrated device. The promise of a $50 entry-level phone became increasingly implausible as engineering costs mounted.

Market Reality

Consumer research revealed a sobering truth: most users weren't interested in customizing hardware. The target audience of tech enthusiasts willing to hand-pick modules was too niche to justify the enormous investment required to build a global supply chain and developer ecosystem from scratch.

Durability Issues

Multiple connection points created potential failure cascades. Water resistance, dust ingress, and drop durability — critical baseline features by 2016 — were nearly impossible to guarantee with modular seams running across every face of the device.

Strategic Refocus

Under new leadership, Google chose to invest in the Pixel smartphone line and AI research. The resources consumed by Ara could deliver faster, more certain returns elsewhere. The project's ambiguity made it an easy target when the company needed to rationalize its hardware bets.

Project Ara faced an insurmountable challenge: the solution it offered didn't match a problem consumers actually had. Modern smartphones had become so capable and affordable that the complexity of modularity outweighed every benefit it promised.

05 / Legacy & Impact

What
It Left Behind

Though Project Ara never reached a single consumer, its influence continues to ripple through the technology industry — in protocols, in philosophy, and in the questions it forced everyone to ask about device ownership.

The Greybus protocol developed for Ara was later adopted in other Google projects, outliving the initiative that created it. The concept directly inspired the "right to repair" movement, fuelling legislative efforts across the US, EU, and beyond that are only now reaching meaningful policy outcomes.

Companies like Fairphone have since shipped modular devices proving there remains a niche market for repairable, sustainable technology. Motorola carried the modular torch briefly with hot-swappable Moto Mods, while enthusiast-grade hardware like Framework's modular laptops demonstrate that Ara's core philosophy found fertile ground in adjacent markets.

Lesson 01

Innovation Requires Iteration

Not every bold idea will succeed, but exploring ambitious concepts advances the entire industry — often in directions nobody anticipated. Ara's failures informed a decade of hardware design thinking.

Lesson 02

User Needs Must Lead

Technical brilliance must align with actual consumer demand. Ara solved an engineering challenge that most users didn't experience as a problem — a fatal mismatch that no amount of engineering excellence could overcome.

Lesson 03

Sustainability Is Complex

While the goal of reducing e-waste was genuinely noble, solutions must also be practical and economically viable for both manufacturer and consumer. Good intentions don't survive bad unit economics.

Lesson 04

Timing Is Everything

Perhaps Project Ara was simply ahead of its time — or perhaps the market will never be ready for full modularity at the consumer level. The difference between a visionary and a failure is often just a decade.
